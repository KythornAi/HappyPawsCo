5 Signs Your Dog is Bored (And It's Not Just Zooms)

You've probably seen your dog tear around the living room like they've been possessed, bouncing off furniture in what can only be described as pure chaos. Most of us call these episodes "the zooms," and they're often a sign of pent-up energy or excitement. But what about the quieter signs that your dog is genuinely bored? The ones that don't involve your sofa becoming a launching pad? Boredom in dogs isn't always loud or obvious, and recognising the subtler signals can help you address the issue before it becomes a bigger problem.

A bored dog isn't just looking for something to do. They're often understimulated mentally, even if they're getting regular walks. This can lead to frustration, anxiety, and behaviours that feel like they've come out of nowhere. Let's look at five signs your dog might be bored, and what you can do to help them feel more engaged and content.

Destructive Chewing on Furniture or Belongings

If you've come home to find your favourite shoes shredded or the corner of the coffee table gnawed, your first thought might be that your dog is being naughty. But destructive chewing is often a sign of boredom rather than deliberate mischief. Dogs need mental stimulation, and when they don't get it, they'll find their own entertainment. Unfortunately, that entertainment often involves your belongings.

Puppies chew as part of teething, but adult dogs who suddenly start destroying things are usually telling you they need more to do. This is especially common in intelligent, high-energy breeds who thrive on problem-solving and activity. If your dog is left alone for long periods without enrichment, chewing becomes a way to pass the time and relieve frustration.

One way to address this is by providing appropriate outlets for chewing, like durable toys or puzzle feeders. The Lick Mat for Dogs can be a brilliant distraction, offering mental stimulation through licking, which releases calming endorphins. Spread a bit of xylitol-free peanut butter or plain yogurt on it, and your dog has a soothing activity that keeps them occupied. It's also worth rotating toys regularly so your dog doesn't lose interest in what's available.

Excessive Barking or Whining

Some dogs are naturally more vocal than others, but if your dog has started barking or whining more than usual, boredom could be the culprit. This is their way of expressing frustration or trying to get your attention. It's not always about wanting to go outside or needing food. Sometimes, they're just saying, "I'm bored, and I need something to do."

This behaviour can be particularly challenging if you're working from home or trying to concentrate on something. Your dog isn't trying to annoy you, but they've run out of ways to entertain themselves. Without mental or physical stimulation, they'll vocalise their dissatisfaction in the hope that you'll step in and provide something more interesting.

If your dog is barking out of boredom, increasing their daily enrichment can help. This doesn't always mean longer walks, though exercise is important. Mental stimulation, like training sessions, sniff games, or interactive toys, can tire them out just as effectively. Short, frequent bursts of engagement throughout the day often work better than one long walk followed by hours of nothing.

Pacing, Restlessness, or Inability to Settle

A dog who can't seem to settle down, pacing from room to room or constantly shifting positions, is often dealing with boredom. This restlessness can look like anxiety, and in some cases, the two overlap. But if your dog has no other signs of stress and simply seems unable to relax, they're likely understimulated and don't know what to do with themselves.

This behaviour is common in dogs who've had a change in routine. Maybe you've gone back to the office after working from home, or the weather's been too miserable for your usual long walks. Dogs thrive on predictability and mental engagement, and when those things are disrupted, restlessness can follow. They're not being difficult. They're just struggling to cope with the lack of activity.

Providing a structured routine can help, as can introducing calming activities. The Slow Feeder Bowl turns mealtime into a mental challenge, extending the time your dog spends eating and giving them something to focus on. It's a simple way to add enrichment without requiring much effort on your part. You might also try short training sessions or scent games to give your dog's brain a workout.

Following You Everywhere (Even to the Bathroom)

It's flattering when your dog wants to be near you, but if they're glued to your side every second of the day, it might be a sign of boredom rather than affection. Dogs who lack independent activities often rely on their owners for entertainment, and when you're not actively engaging with them, they don't know what else to do. This can create a cycle where your dog becomes overly dependent on your presence.

This behaviour is particularly common in dogs who don't have enough to occupy themselves when you're busy. They're not necessarily anxious, though separation issues can develop if the behaviour isn't addressed. They've simply learnt that you're the most interesting thing in the house, and they'd rather follow you than do anything else.

Teaching your dog to settle independently is important. This doesn't mean ignoring them, but it does mean giving them something to do that doesn't involve you. A lick mat, a stuffed Kong, or even a safe chew toy can help them learn to relax on their own. Start with short periods where your dog is occupied while you're in the same room, then gradually increase the distance. It's about building their confidence that they can entertain themselves.

Digging in the Garden or Scratching at Doors

If your garden looks like a construction site or your dog is constantly scratching at doors, they're probably bored. Digging is a natural behaviour for many dogs, but when it becomes excessive, it's often a sign they need more to do. The same goes for scratching at doors or trying to get into areas that are off-limits. They're not being defiant. They're looking for something, anything, to break up the monotony of their day.

This behaviour can be frustrating, especially if you've put effort into maintaining your outdoor space. But punishing your dog for digging or scratching won't solve the problem. They'll just find another outlet for their boredom. Instead, focus on giving them appropriate activities that channel their energy in a more constructive way.

If your dog loves digging, consider creating a designated digging spot in the garden where they're allowed to go to town. Bury toys or treats to make it more rewarding. For dogs who scratch at doors, increasing their daily exercise and mental stimulation can reduce the behaviour. Puzzle toys, scent work, and training sessions are all excellent ways to keep them engaged. You might also try rotating their toys so they always have something "new" to explore.

How to Keep Your Dog Mentally Stimulated

Preventing boredom isn't just about tiring your dog out physically. Mental stimulation is just as important, and in some cases, it's even more effective. A dog who's had a good mental workout will often settle more easily than one who's only had a long walk. The key is variety. Dogs, like people, can get bored with the same routine day after day.

Training sessions are one of the easiest ways to provide mental stimulation. Even five minutes of practising basic commands or teaching a new trick can make a difference. Scent games are another great option. Hide treats around the house or garden and let your dog use their nose to find them. It taps into their natural instincts and gives them a job to do.

Interactive toys and feeders are also brilliant for keeping dogs occupied. The Slow Feeder Bowl turns mealtime into a puzzle, encouraging your dog to work for their food rather than gulping it down in seconds. It's a simple change that adds enrichment to their daily routine. You can also try freezing wet food or treats in a Kong for a longer-lasting challenge.

Variety in walks can help too. Instead of taking the same route every day, explore new areas where your dog can sniff and investigate different scents. Let them lead the walk occasionally, following their nose rather than sticking to a strict path. These small changes can make walks feel more engaging and less like a chore.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a dog be bored even if they get daily walks?

Yes, absolutely. Physical exercise is important, but mental stimulation is just as crucial for keeping dogs happy and balanced. A dog can have a long walk and still feel bored if they're not being challenged mentally. Incorporating training, scent games, or puzzle toys into their routine can help address this.

How much mental stimulation does my dog need each day?

It varies depending on your dog's breed, age, and personality. High-energy breeds and working dogs often need more mental stimulation than calmer, less active breeds. As a general rule, aim for at least 10 to 15 minutes of focused mental activity each day, alongside their regular exercise. This could be training, puzzle toys, or scent work.

Is it normal for my dog to sleep most of the day?

Dogs do sleep a lot, often 12 to 14 hours a day for adult dogs, and even more for puppies and seniors. However, if your dog seems lethargic or disinterested in activities they usually enjoy, it's worth checking in with your vet. Sometimes what looks like boredom can actually be a sign of an underlying health issue.

Can boredom lead to anxiety in dogs?

Yes, chronic boredom can contribute to anxiety, especially if your dog doesn't have healthy outlets for their energy and frustration. Over time, understimulation can lead to stress, which may manifest as destructive behaviour, excessive barking, or even separation issues. Addressing boredom early can help prevent these problems from developing.

Summary: Boredom in dogs isn't always obvious, but signs like destructive chewing, excessive barking, restlessness, clinginess, and digging are all indicators that your dog needs more mental and physical stimulation. Addressing these behaviours with enrichment activities, training, and variety in their routine can help keep them happy and engaged.
