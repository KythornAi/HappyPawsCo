Protecting Your Dog's Paws: Seasonal Hazards & Care

Your dog's paws are tougher than you might think, but they're not invincible. Whether you're navigating winter salt on Manchester pavements or scorching summer tarmac in the Lake District car park, those pads face different challenges throughout the year. Most of us don't think twice about our dog's paws until something goes wrong, but a little seasonal awareness can save your pup a lot of discomfort.

This guide walks you through the main hazards your dog's paws face across the seasons, what signs to watch for, and how to keep those pads healthy year-round. You'll find practical advice that fits into everyday life, whether you're in a city flat or a countryside cottage.

Why Paw Care Matters More Than You'd Think

Your dog's paws do an enormous amount of work. They absorb impact with every step, provide traction on different surfaces, and help regulate body temperature through sweat glands. Unlike our feet, which we protect with shoes, your dog's pads are in direct contact with whatever surface they're walking on.

Damaged paws can lead to limping, reluctance to walk, and in severe cases, infection. What starts as a small crack or burn can quickly become painful if left unchecked. The good news is that most paw problems are preventable with a bit of seasonal awareness and some simple care habits.

Winter Hazards: Salt, Ice, and Cold Surfaces

Winter brings some of the harshest conditions for dog paws, particularly in towns and cities where gritting is common. Rock salt and de-icing chemicals can irritate and crack paw pads, whilst ice itself can cause cuts and abrasions. The cold also makes pads more brittle and prone to splitting.

Snow might seem soft, but it can ball up between your dog's toes, causing discomfort and even frostbite in extreme cases. Dogs with longer fur between their pads are particularly prone to this. You'll notice them stopping to chew at their paws or lifting them awkwardly.

What to Watch For

Check your dog's paws after every winter walk for redness, cracking, or signs of irritation. If they're licking their paws excessively or limping, the salt or cold may be causing problems. Some dogs will refuse to walk on heavily gritted pavements altogether, which is their way of telling you it hurts.

Rinse your dog's paws with lukewarm water when you get home to remove any salt or grit. This simple habit prevents them from licking chemicals off their pads later. Having Pet Wipes in your hallway makes this even easier for quick clean-ups without needing a full rinse. These unscented wipes are hypoallergenic and contain soothing ingredients like aloe and chamomile.

Protection Options

Paw balm applied before walks creates a protective barrier against salt and cold. Look for natural ingredients like beeswax or shea butter. Some owners use dog boots, though not every dog tolerates them. If your dog will wear them, they're the most effective protection for winter walks.

Stick to less-gritted routes where possible. Grass verges and park paths are gentler than heavily salted pavements. Early morning walks might mean more ice but less salt, whilst later in the day the opposite is true. You'll need to weigh up which is worse for your particular dog.

Summer Hazards: Hot Pavements and Rough Terrain

Summer brings a completely different set of challenges. Hot tarmac, concrete, and sand can burn your dog's pads surprisingly quickly. If the pavement feels too hot for the back of your hand held there for five seconds, it's too hot for your dog's paws.

Rough terrain like gravel paths, rocky trails, and stubble fields can also cause abrasions, particularly if your dog isn't used to those surfaces. A countryside walk that's fine in winter can become uncomfortable in summer when the ground is hard and dry.

Timing Your Walks

Walk early in the morning or late in the evening during hot weather. Pavements retain heat for hours after the sun goes down, so even an 8pm walk can be risky on a scorching day. Grass and dirt paths stay much cooler than tarmac and concrete.

Always carry water on summer walks. Dehydration affects paw health too, as dry pads are more prone to cracking. The Collapsible Travel Bowls clip easily to your bag or lead and include a bottle carrier, so you can offer water regularly without carrying bulky equipment.

Recognising Burns

If your dog suddenly starts limping or refusing to walk, check their paws immediately. Burns appear as darkened, damaged areas on the pads, and your dog will likely lick them persistently. In severe cases, blisters can form. If you suspect a burn, rinse the paw with cool water and contact your vet.

Some dogs will try to walk on grass verges or in the shade to avoid hot surfaces. Don't ignore this behaviour. They're telling you the ground is uncomfortable. Carry them to a cooler surface if needed or head home and try again later.

Spring and Autumn: Mud, Thorns, and Allergens

Spring and autumn might seem gentler on paws, but they bring their own challenges. Muddy conditions can hide sharp objects like glass, stones, or thorns. Brambles and thistles are particularly common on overgrown paths, and your dog can easily pick up thorns between their pads.

Pollen and grass seeds also become problematic in spring. Grass seeds can work their way into paw pads, causing painful infections. You'll notice your dog licking one paw obsessively or limping. Check between their toes carefully after walks through long grass.

Post-Walk Checks

Get into the habit of checking your dog's paws after every walk, particularly during muddy season. Look between the toes for embedded objects, cuts, or swelling. Wipe away mud before it dries and hardens, as this makes it easier to spot problems.

If you find a thorn or grass seed that's embedded deeply or causing swelling, don't try to remove it yourself. Your vet can extract it properly and check for infection. Superficial thorns that haven't penetrated deeply can usually be removed with clean tweezers.

Year-Round Paw Care Habits

Beyond seasonal hazards, some basic habits keep your dog's paws healthier throughout the year. Regular nail trimming prevents nails from pushing pads into awkward positions. Overgrown nails change how your dog walks and can cause long-term joint problems.

Keep the fur between your dog's pads trimmed short. This reduces the risk of snow balling, prevents mud from matting, and makes it easier to spot injuries. Most groomers will do this as part of a regular trim, or you can learn to do it carefully at home with blunt-ended scissors.

Building Paw Toughness

Dogs who walk regularly on varied surfaces develop tougher pads naturally. If your dog spends most of their time on soft grass or carpet, their pads will be more sensitive. Gradually introducing different surfaces helps, but don't overdo it. A sudden long walk on rough ground can cause soreness even in active dogs.

Older dogs often develop thinner, more sensitive pads as they age. You might notice they're more reluctant to walk on rough surfaces than they used to be. Adjust your routes accordingly and consider shorter, more frequent walks on gentler terrain.

When to See Your Vet

Most minor paw injuries heal with basic first aid and rest, but some situations need professional attention. Contact your vet if your dog is limping persistently, if you notice swelling or discharge, or if they're in obvious pain when you touch their paw.

Deep cuts, burns, or embedded objects that you can't remove safely also warrant a vet visit. Don't wait to see if it gets better on its own. Paw infections can develop quickly and become serious if left untreated.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I use paw balm every day or only before walks?

It depends on your dog's pads and the conditions. If your dog's pads are healthy and you're walking on moderate surfaces, applying balm before potentially harsh walks is usually enough. Dogs with naturally dry or cracked pads may benefit from daily application, particularly in winter. Over-application isn't usually harmful, but it can make floors slippery indoors, so wipe off excess before coming inside.

My dog hates having their paws touched. How can I check them properly?

Start slowly with positive associations. Touch one paw briefly whilst giving treats, then gradually increase the duration. Make it part of your routine after pleasant activities like meals or play. Some dogs tolerate paw handling better when they're tired after a walk. If your dog remains extremely resistant, ask your vet or a trainer for desensitisation techniques. Regular handling from puppyhood makes this much easier, but older dogs can learn to tolerate it too.

Are dog boots worth the hassle, or is paw balm enough?

Boots provide the best protection in extreme conditions, but they only work if your dog will wear them. Many dogs refuse or walk strangely in boots, which defeats the purpose. Paw balm is a good middle ground for most situations. Reserve boots for severe winter conditions, very hot pavements, or dogs with existing paw injuries. Introduce boots gradually at home with treats before expecting your dog to walk in them outdoors.

Can I use human moisturiser on my dog's paws?

No, avoid human moisturisers, lotions, or creams. Dogs lick their paws, and many human products contain ingredients that are harmful if ingested. Some also contain fragrances or additives that can irritate paw pads. Use products specifically formulated for dogs, which are safe if licked and designed for the tougher skin on paw pads.

How do I know if a paw injury needs a vet or will heal on its own?

Minor abrasions, small cuts that aren't bleeding heavily, and mild redness usually heal with rest and keeping the area clean. See your vet if there's persistent limping beyond 24 hours, visible swelling, discharge, a bad smell, or if your dog won't let you touch the paw at all. Deep cuts, puncture wounds, burns, or anything embedded in the paw needs professional attention. When in doubt, call your vet and describe what you're seeing. They can advise whether it needs an appointment.

Summary: Your dog's paws face different hazards throughout the year, from winter salt and ice to summer heat and spring thorns. Regular checks after walks, seasonal adjustments to routes and timing, and basic protective measures keep those pads healthy and comfortable year-round.
